About Me

I am Ga Eun Cho (조가은, pronounced: GAH-un JOH). I am a PhD candidate in Political Science at Johns Hopkins University and a Graduate Fellow at the Chloe Center for the Critical Study of Racism, Immigration, and Colonialism. This year, I am conducting dissertation fieldwork as a Junior Fellow at the International Center for Korean Studies in the Kyujanggak Institute at Seoul National University.


My dissertation, tentatively titled "Rethinking Migration and Development - The Politics of Emigration in South Korea," examines state-led emigration and diaspora engagement policies as strategies of economic development.


My broader research interests span East Asian politics, political economy, international relations with particular focus on borders and security and migration and citizenship. My work draws on extensive archival research and in-depth interviews in English, Korean, and mixed Chinese-Korean characterizations.


I obtained my MA in International Relations from Seoul National University and a BA in Asian Studies and Economics from Yonsei University.

Research Areas

Political Economy of Migration

Why do states encourage or discourage migration for economic development? I examine how countries design and execute migration policies such as immigrant incorporation, mass emigration, such as diaspora engagement, according to their larger strategies of economic development.

Borders and Security

Why do states control movement across borders for national security? I examine different tools of mobility control, such as border control, and passport and visa regulations to understand how states frame and execute border security in a globalized world.

East Asian Politics and Society

I examine political and social transformations in East Asia, such as state-society relations, institutional development, and transitional justice, with particular expertise in the Korean Peninsula.
