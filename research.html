Research

Working Papers

Exile by Paperwork: Ambiguity as Diaspora Governance in Divided Post-Colonial States [+]
Draft available on request
Abstract How do states govern diasporas simultaneously claimed by a rival sovereign? The global processes of decolonization and the Cold War produced "divided states," such as the Republic of Korea (South Korea) and the Democratic People's Republic of Korea (North Korea), each claiming sole legitimate representation of a single nation and its diaspora. Yet the study of diaspora governance in International Relations largely assumes a single home state that includes or excludes external populations on economic, security, or normative grounds — overlooking how structural conditions like division reshape diaspora governance. In divided states, neither full exclusion nor full incorporation of diasporas is viable: exclusion risks ceding legitimacy to the rival, while incorporation of populations tied to the rival poses ideological and security threats. I argue that divided states resolve this dilemma by maintaining the ambiguous status of diasporic populations. Drawing on South Korea's diaspora policies from division to the present, this article identifies a mechanism I term "exile by paperwork": the strategic use of bureaucratic instruments of belonging and mobility to effectively exclude populations without formally disavowing them. By keeping legal status revocable and uncertain, the state places targeted populations in a condition of permanent suspension, using the threat of exclusion and the promise of inclusion as tools of discipline. This contributes to scholarship on diaspora governance by showing how bureaucratic power operates not only through legibility, but through the deliberate production of ambiguity amid geopolitical competition.

Book Chapters

Eisenhower Administration's Foreign Policy Decision-Making Process in Response to Syngman Rhee's Release of Anti-Communist Prisoners [+]
In Chaesung Chun (Ed.), Generality and Specificity in East Asia Regional Order: Theory and Reality. Seoul: Sapyong, 2018.
Abstract This chapter analyzes why the Eisenhower administration decided to compromise with Syngman Rhee's brinkmanship strategy of releasing anti-communist prisoners during the Korean War, and agreed to sign the Mutual Defense Treaty Between the United States and Korea. It examines the perceptions of five key decision-makers — President Eisenhower, Secretary of State Dulles, Assistant Secretary of State for Far Eastern Affairs Robertson, US Ambassador to Korea Briggs, and UNC Commander-in-Chief Clark — over the period from the release of anti-communist prisoners on June 19, 1953 to the signing of NSC 154/1 on July 3, 1953, which promised the Mutual Defense Treaty. The chapter concludes that decision-makers' perceptions of Rhee's intentions and rationale were integral to the outcome.

Thesis

Formulation and Systemization of Transnational Adoption Policy During Park Chung-Hee's Regime [+]
MA Thesis, Seoul National University, 2019.
Abstract In the aftermath of the Korean War, South Korea aggressively promoted transnational adoption as an alternative to social welfare for children in need. The Park Chung-hee administration established Korea's transnational adoption policy with the 1961 "Intercountry Adoption Law with Regard to the Orphans," and continued to send children abroad despite international scrutiny in the 1970s. This study argues that it was under the Park Chung-hee regime that transnational adoption became systemized in Korea, tracing the relationship between the government's modernization discourse, the collusion of Korean society, international demand for adoption, and the growth of adoption agencies. The government's modernization discourse — defining progress solely through economic growth — shaped which populations were treated as contributing to that goal and which were not, discriminating against the latter in ways whose effects continue today.
